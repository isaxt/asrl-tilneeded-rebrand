The problem

Supply chains break exactly when you need them most

During the COVID-19 pandemic, buyers with signed distributor contracts discovered what allocation clauses really mean: orders canceled, reduced, or rerouted without notice, at the peak of need. Prices and terms stay hidden behind non-disclosure agreements. Intermediaries capture the spread between what factories earn and what buyers pay — while adding no value to the product itself.

The result is a supply chain in which no buyer or factory can plan with confidence. Stockpiles expire in warehouses. Just-in-time inventories collapse in disruptions. And the information needed to fix it — real prices, real capacity, real terms — is contractually concealed.

The mechanism

One contract. Two parties. No middle layer.

A TilNeeded Contract is a direct agreement between a factory and an end-user buyer that locks price, quantity, and delivery speed. Two election modes cover every purchase:

Buy Now

Immediate purchase, factory-direct

The buyer purchases at factory-direct pricing with contractually secured delivery terms. No distributor markup, no allocation clause, no NDA.

RCR Election

Reserve capacity, purchase when needed

The buyer pays a negotiated Reserve Capacity Reservation (RCR) Fee — 10% is the starting point — to hold the factory's capacity at locked terms, typically for a year. Trigger the purchase only when the product is actually needed.

Both parties are bound equally by a mutual hell-or-high-water performance obligation, enforceable under UCC Article 2. The factory cannot abandon delivery; the buyer cannot withdraw once a purchase is triggered. Fairness is not an aspiration — it is written into the contract.

Transparency by design

Every term public. Every party visible.

On the TilNeeded Platform, contract price, quantity, delivery speed, and country of origin are publicly searchable. No NDAs. No allocation clauses. Financial transactions occur off-platform — the platform is a transparency registry and community, not a financial intermediary.

Factories may elect to disclose per-item cost. Buyers may elect a Worker Consideration — an hourly contribution to factory workers. Both elections are public.
